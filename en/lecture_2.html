Structural Bioinformatics

Sequence is not everything! The function of a protein in a cell depends primarily on its unique three-dimensional shape.

Structural bioinformatics deals with: - Analysis, visualization, and comparison of 3D structures. - Predicting structures based solely on the amino acid sequence. - Simulating interactions (e.g., virtual docking of new drugs).

Levels of Protein Organization

  • Primary Structure: The amino acid sequence (the order of aa in the chain).
  • Secondary Structure: Local spatial folding of the chain (alpha-helices and beta-sheets) stabilized by hydrogen bonds.
  • Tertiary Structure: The complete three-dimensional folding of a single protein chain.
  • Quaternary Structure: A complex made up of several subunits (separate chains).

Where do structures come from? (Experimental Methods)

Determining a structure in a laboratory is difficult and expensive. Main techniques:

  1. X-ray Crystallography (~90% of structures) – requires growing a protein crystal and analyzing the diffraction pattern under X-rays.
  2. Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR, ~9% of structures) – allows studying proteins in solution (closer to natural conditions).
  3. Cryo-Electron Microscopy (Cryo-EM) – the revolution of recent years, enabling the study of massive protein complexes without crystallization.

Protein Data Bank (PDB)

The world’s main repository for 3D structures of macromolecules!

  • It contains structures of proteins, nucleic acids, and their complexes.
  • Despite an impressive number of entries (over 200,000), it’s still a fraction compared to the hundreds of millions of known sequences in UniProt.
  • Each structure receives its unique identifier (e.g., 1YE3).

PDB Format: How is a structure saved?

A structure file is not an image – it’s a text file containing 3D coordinates!

Key sections: - ATOM: The \(x, y, z\) coordinates of the main atoms building the protein’s amino acids. - HETATM: Atoms of non-standard “residues”, e.g., water molecules, inhibitors, or prosthetic groups. - B-factor: Temperature factor – tells us about the thermal mobility of a given atom and the quality of the experiment in that region.

Visualization Tools

To turn the \(x,y,z\) coordinates into a beautiful, three-dimensional image on a computer screen, we use specialized software:

  • UCSF Chimera / ChimeraX
  • PyMOL
  • VMD
  • Rasmol

Hierarchical Classification of Folds

Just as we categorize living organisms, we can categorize the shapes of protein domains into evolutionary trees. Two main systems are:

  • CATH: (Class, Architecture, Topology, Homologous superfamily)
  • SCOP: (Class, Fold, Superfamily, Family, Domain)

The main division is based on the elements (alpha-helices or beta-sheets) the protein is built of.

Structure Prediction: When experiment fails

Since we know the genetic sequence of a protein from NGS, how can we know its structure without going to the lab?

Main computational approaches: 1. Ab initio (de novo) 2. Fold Recognition (Threading) 3. Homology Modeling

Classical Methods: Ab initio and Threading

  • Ab initio: Modeling based on the laws of physics and interatomic interactions. Theoretically ideal, practically limited by computational power (only for the smallest proteins).
  • Threading (Fold Recognition): We take our sequence and algorithmically “thread” it onto all known spatial shapes from the PDB, checking which “skeleton” fits it best.

Homology Modeling

The gold standard for decades before AI arrived. Key assumption: During evolution, the spatial structure of a protein is much more conserved (unchanging) than its amino acid sequence.

Steps: 1. Finding a homologous protein with a known structure (template). 2. Performing sequence alignment. 3. Building a 3D model superimposed on the template. 4. Model evaluation.

How to assess model quality? (Validation)

How to check if the computer-generated structure makes chemical sense at all?

This is where the torsion angles (\(\Phi\), \(\Psi\), \(\omega\)) of peptide bonds come in. Steric hindrances (“spatial clashes”) mean that amino acids can only bend at certain angles.

The famous Ramachandran Plot is used to check this correctness! If atoms “overlap”, we will see it on the plot. Another popular parameter is RMSD (Root-Mean-Square Deviation).

The Revolution: AlphaFold

What took months of calculations and yielded uncertain results from homology modeling for decades was solved in 2020 by DeepMind (Google).

  • The system predicts 3D structures with accuracy approaching experimental methods.
  • It is based on Neural Networks and incorporates the co-evolution of amino acids from Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA).
  • Today, most of biology relies on virtual structures calculated by this AI!
